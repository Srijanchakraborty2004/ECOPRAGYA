Effects of Climate changes

Climate change is our planet’s greatest existential threat. If we don’t limit greenhouse gas emissions from the burning of fossil fuels, the consequences of rising global temperatures include massive crop and fishery collapse, the disappearance of hundreds of thousands of species, and entire communities becoming uninhabitable. While these outcomes may still be avoidable, climate change is already causing suffering and death. From raging wildfires and supercharged storms, its compounding effects can be felt today, outside our own windows

Effects of climate change on weather

As global temperatures climb, widespread shifts in weather systems occur, making events like droughts, hurricanes, and floods more intense and unpredictable. Extreme weather events that may have hit just once in our grandparents’ lifetimes are becoming more common in ours. However, not every place will experience the same effects: Climate change may cause severe drought in one region while making floods more likely in another. Already, the planet has warmed 1.1 degrees Celsius (1.9 degrees Fahrenheit) since the preindustrial era began 250 years ago, according to the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC). And scientists warn it could reach a worst-case scenario of 4 degrees Celsius (7.2 degrees Fahrenheit) by 2100 if we fail to tackle the causes of climate change—namely, the burning of fossil fuels (coal, oil, and gas)

Effects of climate change on humans

Ultimately, the way climate change impacts weather, the environment, animals, and agriculture affects humanity as well. But there’s more. Around the world, our ways of life—from how we get our food to the industries around which our economies are based—have all developed in the context of relatively stable climates. As global warming shakes this foundation, it promises to alter the very fabric of society. At worst, this could lead to widespread famine, disease, war, displacement, injury, and death. For many around the world, this grim forecast is already their reality. In this way, climate change poses an existential threat to all human life..

Here is a brief overview of various effects of climate change:

Sea level Rise

Sea level rise refers to the increase in global ocean levels due to climate change, primarily caused by melting glaciers and ice sheets, and thermal expansion of seawater. This rise poses significant threats to coastal ecosystems, cities, and communities, including flooding, erosion, and saltwater intrusion into freshwater sources.

Temperature Rise

Global temperature rise refers to the long-term increase in Earth's average surface temperature, primarily caused by human-induced greenhouse gas emissions. Since 1880, the average temperature has risen by 1.1°C (2.0°F), with the 20 warmest years occurring since 1981, and the top five warmest years since 2016.

Intanse Heat wave

Intense heat waves refer to prolonged periods of abnormally hot weather, typically exceeding 5-7 days, with temperatures significantly higher than average. They can lead to heat-related illnesses, increased mortality, and infrastructure damage. Heat waves are exacerbated by climate change, urbanization, and humidity, posing significant threats to human health and ecosystems.

Extreme Weather Events

Extreme weather events refer to severe and uncommon weather conditions, such as hurricanes, droughts, floods, wildfires, and heatwaves, that exceed normal variability and cause significant impacts on human life, infrastructure, and the environment. Climate change is increasing the frequency, intensity, and unpredictability of these events, leading to devastating consequences.

Longer Wildfire Season

A longer wildfire season refers to the extended period of time when conditions are ripe for wildfires to ignite and spread, typically due to drought, heat, and dry vegetation. Climate change has led to an increase in wildfire season length, now often spanning 6-12 months in some regions, posing greater risks to lives, property, and ecosystems.

Instense Cyclone

An intense cyclone is a powerful, rotating storm with sustained winds exceeding 119 km/h (74 mph). Characterized by strong winds, heavy rainfall, and storm surges, intense cyclones can cause catastrophic damage, flooding, and loss of life. Climate change is projected to increase the frequency and intensity of these storms.

Biodiversity loss

Climate change is driving biodiversity loss by altering ecosystems, disrupting species' habitats, and exacerbating extinction risk. Rising temperatures, changing precipitation patterns, and increased frequency of extreme events impact species' adaptability, leading to population declines, community shifts, and loss of ecosystem resilience, compromising the health and functionality of ecosystems.

Mortality

Climate change is linked to increased mortality through extreme weather events, heat stress, air pollution, and altered disease patterns. Warmer temperatures can lead to cardiovascular and respiratory issues, while extreme events like hurricanes, floods, and wildfires cause direct fatalities. Climate-related mortality is projected to rise, disproportionately affecting vulnerable populations.
